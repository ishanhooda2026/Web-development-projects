CAT. 01CLASS: STELLAR EVOLUTIONCONFIDENCE: HIGH

A star's death is decided
before it's born

The single number that determines whether a star ends as a slowly cooling ember, an exposed atomic nucleus the size of a city, or a hole in spacetime is fixed the moment it starts fusing hydrogen: its mass.

The Chandrasekhar limit

A white dwarf is held up not by heat but by electron degeneracy pressure — a purely quantum-mechanical refusal of electrons to occupy the same state, described by the Pauli exclusion principle. Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar showed in 1930, at age 19, that this pressure can only support about 1.4 solar masses. Push a white dwarf past that line — by feeding it matter from a companion star — and electron degeneracy fails catastrophically, igniting runaway carbon fusion across the whole star in seconds. The result, a Type Ia supernova, is bright enough to briefly outshine its host galaxy, and because it always detonates at almost exactly the same mass, it always releases almost exactly the same energy. That consistency is what let astronomers use them as "standard candles" to discover the universe's expansion is accelerating.

Core collapse in under a second

A massive star spends millions of years fusing hydrogen, then helium, then progressively heavier elements in onion-like shells — each stage faster than the last, because heavier fuels release less energy per reaction. Silicon burning to iron can finish in a single day. Iron cannot be fused for a net energy gain, so fusion stops, the core's support vanishes, and roughly 1.5 solar masses of matter collapses from the size of Earth to the size of a city — about 30 km — in under a quarter of a second. The rebound of infalling outer layers off that suddenly rigid core drives the supernova shockwave.

Where your calcium came from

Every element heavier than iron on the periodic table — gold, iodine, uranium — was almost certainly forged in less than a second, during a supernova's core collapse or a neutron star merger, via rapid neutron capture (the r-process). Calcium and oxygen, on the other hand, are cooked slowly over a star's lifetime in nuclear fusion. The literal atoms of bone and blood in your body were assembled on two totally different timescales, by two totally different kinds of stellar violence, and then scattered across the galaxy long before the Sun existed.

A teaspoon that weighs a mountain

Electron degeneracy pressure only wins up to the Chandrasekhar limit. Beyond about 1.4–3 solar masses, gravity forces electrons and protons to merge into neutrons, and the remnant becomes a neutron star, held up instead by neutron degeneracy pressure. The result is matter denser than anything else that isn't a black hole: a teaspoon of neutron-star material would weigh roughly as much as a mountain — on the order of a billion tonnes. It is one continuous atomic nucleus, 20 km across, held together by gravity rather than the strong force.

CAT. 02CLASS: COMPACT & RELATIVISTIC OBJECTSCONFIDENCE: HIGH–SPECULATIVE

Objects where physics
stops behaving

Neutron stars, magnetars, and black holes aren't just "extreme versions" of normal stars — past a certain point, the equations governing them switch from Newtonian gravity to full general relativity, and intuition stops being useful.

Magnetars: the strongest magnets known

A magnetar is a neutron star with a magnetic field of roughly 1011 tesla — for comparison, a hospital MRI machine runs at about 1–3 tesla. A field that strong would distort the electron clouds of atoms into cigar shapes and could, in principle, disrupt matter's chemistry from a thousand kilometers away. Starquakes on magnetars — sudden crustal fractures — release more energy in a fraction of a second than the Sun emits in 100,000 years, and were caught by satellites designed to watch for nuclear weapons tests, because they briefly outshine everything else in gamma rays.

Pulsars: lighthouses by accident

A pulsar is a rotating neutron star whose magnetic axis is misaligned with its spin axis, sweeping a beam of radio waves past Earth like a lighthouse. The first one discovered, in 1967 by Jocelyn Bell Burnell, was nicknamed "LGM-1" — Little Green Men — because its pulses were so regular that a natural origin seemed briefly implausible. Some pulsars spin over 700 times per second; their surfaces move at a meaningful fraction of the speed of light.

The photon sphere

Around any black hole there is a spherical shell — for a non-rotating hole, at 1.5 times the Schwarzschild radius — where spacetime is curved so sharply that light itself can travel in a closed circular orbit. It's unstable; any tiny perturbation sends the photon spiraling in or escaping. This is also the physical mechanism behind the bright "photon ring" seen in the Event Horizon Telescope images of M87* and Sagittarius A*: it is light that has looped the black hole one or more times before escaping to our cameras.

Spaghettification is a real, calculable event

Tidal force scales with the difference in gravitational pull between your head and feet. For a stellar-mass black hole, that difference becomes lethal thousands of kilometers before the event horizon — the mechanism astronomers actually call spaghettification. Counterintuitively, for a supermassive black hole like Sagittarius A*, the event horizon is so large and gently curved that tidal forces at the horizon itself are survivable; you could, in principle, cross the horizon of a large enough black hole without immediately being torn apart — though there is no way back out to report on what came next.

Black holes are the simplest objects in physics

The "no-hair theorem" states that an isolated black hole is completely described by just three numbers: mass, electric charge, and angular momentum. Everything else about the matter that fell in — its composition, shape, history — is lost to any outside observer. A black hole made of collapsed stars and one made of collapsed bicycles, at the same mass and spin, are indistinguishable from the outside. This "loss" of information is the actual heart of the black hole information paradox, not the destruction of matter itself.

Hawking radiation and cosmic old age

Quantum field theory predicts that black holes slowly evaporate via Hawking radiation, and the smaller the black hole, the faster it evaporates. A stellar-mass black hole would take on the order of 1067 years to evaporate — vastly longer than the current age of the universe, about 1.4 × 1010 years. This single fact is a cornerstone of "heat death" cosmology: black holes will very plausibly outlast every star, galaxy, and planet, making them some of the last structures in the universe to disappear.

CAT. 03CLASS: EXOPLANETARY SCIENCECONFIDENCE: MIXED

We've never seen
most of these worlds

Over 5,000 confirmed exoplanets exist in catalogs today, and the overwhelming majority were never directly imaged. They were inferred — from wobbles, dips, and tiny chemical fingerprints in starlight.

How you "see" a planet by not seeing it

The transit method — used by Kepler and TESS — detects planets by measuring the tiny dimming of starlight as a planet crosses in front of its star, sometimes by as little as 0.01%. It only works for the small fraction of planetary systems oriented edge-on to Earth. The radial velocity method instead detects the star's own wobble, pulled by the planet's gravity, as a periodic Doppler shift in its spectrum — this is how the first exoplanet around a Sun-like star, 51 Pegasi b, was found in 1995.

Reading an atmosphere from light-years away

When a planet transits, a sliver of starlight passes through its atmosphere on the way to us, and different gases absorb specific wavelengths. JWST's spectrographs can detect these absorption fingerprints well enough to identify water vapor, carbon dioxide, and even sulfur dioxide — the byproduct of photochemistry — in the atmospheres of planets tens to hundreds of light-years away, without ever directly resolving the planet as more than a point of light.

Tidally locked doesn't mean dead

Most planets found in the "habitable zones" of small red dwarf stars, including several in the TRAPPIST-1 system, are almost certainly tidally locked — one hemisphere in permanent day, one in permanent night, like the Moon around Earth. Climate models suggest this needn't be sterilizing: strong atmospheric circulation could carry heat from the dayside to the nightside, and the terminator zone — the ring of permanent twilight — is a genuine candidate for the most temperate real estate on such a world.

Worlds with no Earth analog

The most common type of planet found by Kepler doesn't exist in our solar system at all: "sub-Neptunes," 2–4 times Earth's radius with thick hydrogen/helium envelopes over a rocky or icy core. Then there are "hot Jupiters" — gas giants orbiting closer to their star than Mercury orbits the Sun, some with predicted iron rain or winds exceeding 2 km/s — and "ocean worlds" theorized to be covered by liquid water hundreds of kilometers deep, far beyond Earth's average ocean depth of about 3.7 km.

CAT. 04CLASS: COSMOLOGYCONFIDENCE: HIGH FOR PAST, MODEL-DEPENDENT FOR FUTURE

The universe has
a fossil record too

We can't watch the early universe happen. But its afterglow is still arriving, and its geometry still shows scars from its first fraction of a second.

You can watch the Big Bang's afterglow tonight

About 1% of the static on an old analog TV tuned between channels is, in a real sense, the cosmic microwave background — relic photons released about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, when the universe cooled enough for electrons and protons to combine into neutral hydrogen and become transparent to light for the first time. That radiation has been redshifted by expansion from roughly 3,000 K down to 2.7 K, and it arrives from every direction, uniformly, all the time.

Inflation solved a problem you'd never think to ask

The cosmic microwave background is uniform in temperature to about 1 part in 100,000 across regions of sky that, without extremely rapid early expansion, would never have been in causal contact — light wouldn't have had time to travel between them to equalize temperature. Cosmic inflation, a theorized exponential expansion in the universe's first fraction of a second (around 10-32 seconds), is the leading explanation for why the universe looks so thermally uniform on the largest scales.

Dark energy is winning, and it's accelerating

In the late 1990s, observations of distant Type Ia supernovae showed they were dimmer — meaning farther away — than a universe with constant expansion would predict. The universe's expansion is not just continuing, it's accelerating, driven by a poorly understood component dubbed dark energy that makes up roughly 68% of the universe's total energy density. Unlike matter, dark energy's density does not dilute as space expands — which is precisely why it eventually dominates everything else.

Heat death is a genuine long-range forecast

Assuming dark energy behaves as current models predict, the universe's most likely long-term future is not a fiery end but a cold, dark, static one: stars burn out over roughly the next 1014 years, black holes evaporate over roughly 10100 years, and eventually entropy approaches its maximum in a state cosmologists call "heat death" — no more free energy available to do anything, including think.

CAT. 05CLASS: SPACEFLIGHT ENGINEERINGCONFIDENCE: VERIFIED / FLOWN

Getting there is
mostly a trick of momentum

Rockets are the least efficient part of spaceflight. Almost everything interesting that happens after launch is done with orbital mechanics, not thrust.

Stealing momentum from planets

A gravity assist doesn't just "redirect" a spacecraft — it can genuinely speed it up by stealing a tiny amount of orbital momentum from a planet, exchanged through gravity exactly like a slingshot conserving momentum in a collision. Voyager 2's grand tour of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune was only possible because a rare planetary alignment in the late 1970s let each flyby accelerate it toward the next target — an alignment that wouldn't repeat for about 176 years.

The Oberth effect: burn low, burn fast

Firing a rocket engine produces more useful kinetic energy the faster you're already moving, because kinetic energy scales with velocity squared, not linearly with the fuel burned. This means the most efficient place to burn fuel for a big velocity change is at the lowest, fastest point of an orbit — deep in a planet's or the Sun's gravity well — rather than burning gradually. Mission planners deliberately dive spacecraft close to the Sun or a planet specifically to exploit this.

Ion engines: barely any thrust, almost no fuel

An ion thruster accelerates charged xenon atoms with an electric field and expels them at extremely high velocity, producing thrust comparable to the weight of a sheet of paper resting on your hand. But because exhaust velocity — not thrust — determines fuel efficiency, ion engines can be roughly 10 times more fuel-efficient than chemical rockets. NASA's Dawn spacecraft used one to become the first probe to orbit two separate deep-space destinations, Vesta and Ceres, on a fuel budget a chemical rocket could never match.

Aerobraking: using an atmosphere as a free brake

Slowing a spacecraft down with rocket fuel to enter orbit is expensive; skimming a planet's upper atmosphere repeatedly to bleed off velocity through drag is nearly free. Several Mars orbiters have used dozens to hundreds of careful aerobraking passes over weeks to shrink their orbit, trading mission time for a dramatic reduction in the propellant that would otherwise have to be launched from Earth.

Why deep-space probes still run on plutonium

Solar panels lose effectiveness roughly with the inverse square of distance from the Sun, making them impractical beyond about Jupiter's orbit. Missions like Voyager, New Horizons, and Curiosity instead use radioisotope thermoelectric generators — blocks of plutonium-238 whose steady radioactive decay heat is converted directly to electricity via the thermoelectric effect, with no moving parts to fail. Voyager 1's RTGs, launched in 1977, still produce enough power to run a reduced set of instruments today.

CAT. 06CLASS: ASTROBIOLOGY & HUMAN ARTIFACTSCONFIDENCE: MIXED

The line between
"alive" and "space-proof"
is blurrier than expected

Tardigrades survived exposure to open space

In 2007, the European Space Agency's FOTON-M3 mission exposed tardigrades to the vacuum, cosmic radiation, and extreme temperature of open space for roughly 10 days outside a spacecraft, with no protection. A significant fraction survived and went on to reproduce normally after returning to Earth. They achieve this partly by entering a desiccated, near-metabolism-free state called a tun, in which their cells produce protective proteins that effectively vitrify sensitive internal structures.

The Golden Record's needle came with instructions for aliens

Each Voyager probe carries a gold-plated copper phonograph record etched with greetings in 55 languages, natural sounds of Earth, and 116 images encoded as analog waveforms. The cover includes a diagram showing how to build a cartridge and stylus to play it, the correct rotational speed, and a pulsar map — using the precise, predictable timing of known pulsars as literal cosmic coordinates — to indicate Earth's location and the record's launch date to any civilization that might intercept it, conceivably millions of years from now.

Panspermia is a real, testable hypothesis

Lithopanspermia — the idea that microbial life could survive being blasted off a planet inside rock ejecta from an asteroid impact, drift through space for millions of years, and re-seed another world on impact — is not fringe speculation. Extremophile bacteria and their spores have survived simulated impact shock, vacuum, and radiation exposure in laboratory tests, and meteorites confirmed to have originated from Mars have been found on Earth, demonstrating the transport mechanism itself is real even though transported life has never been confirmed.

Life thrives in places we assumed were sterile

Hydrothermal vent ecosystems on the ocean floor, discovered in 1977, run on chemosynthesis rather than sunlight — bacteria oxidize hydrogen sulfide from vent fluid to produce energy, supporting entire food webs of shrimp, crabs, and eyeless fish in total darkness. This discovery directly reshaped the search for extraterrestrial life: Europa and Enceladus, icy moons with subsurface oceans heated by tidal flexing rather than sunlight, are now considered leading astrobiology targets precisely because Earth proved sunlight isn't a prerequisite for a functioning ecosystem.

CAT. 07CLASS: UNSOLVEDCONFIDENCE: OPEN

The log ends here —
these entries have no citation yet

Every fact above is well-attested. These aren't. They're included because the honest state of astrophysics includes several enormous, unresolved gaps.

Dark matter: 85% of the universe's matter, and we've never touched it

Galaxies rotate as if far more mass exists than the visible stars and gas can account for — a discrepancy first flagged by Vera Rubin's rotation curve measurements in the 1970s. The leading explanation is an invisible form of matter that interacts through gravity but not light, making up roughly five-sixths of all matter in the universe. Decades of direct-detection experiments deep underground have failed to capture a single dark matter particle, leaving its actual identity completely open.

The Fermi Paradox has no consensus answer

Given the sheer number of stars and plausible planets in the galaxy, and the amount of time available for a technological civilization to spread across it, physicist Enrico Fermi's informal question — "where is everybody?" — remains unresolved. Proposed answers range from a "Great Filter" that makes some step in life's development from chemistry to interstellar travel astronomically rare, to the possibility that interstellar travel is simply never worth the cost for any civilization, to the more unsettling idea that technological civilizations reliably destroy themselves.

The Wow! signal was never explained

On August 15, 1977, the Big Ear radio telescope recorded a 72-second narrowband radio signal at almost exactly the hydrogen line frequency (1420 MHz) — a frequency considered a likely "universal" hailing frequency because hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe. The signal was strong, matched the shape expected from a fixed celestial source moving through the telescope's field of view, and has never repeated or been explained by any confirmed natural or artificial source since.

The Hubble tension might be a crack in cosmology itself

Two independent methods of measuring how fast the universe is expanding — one using the cosmic microwave background, one using nearby supernovae and stars called Cepheid variables as distance markers — disagree by roughly 8%, far more than either method's stated margin of error should allow. Cosmologists still don't agree whether this "Hubble tension" points to an unaccounted-for measurement error, or to genuinely new physics missing from the standard cosmological model.
